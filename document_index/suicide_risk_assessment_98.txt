This guide provides supportive measures to take while waiting for professional help.*

**Suicide Risk Assessment: A First Aid Guide**

**1. Definition and Symptoms**

*   **Definition:** Suicide is the act of intentionally ending one's own life. Suicide risk assessment involves evaluating the likelihood that an individual will attempt or complete suicide.
*   **Symptoms and Warning Signs:** Recognizing warning signs is crucial. These can be categorized as:
    *   **Direct Verbal Cues:**
        *   "I want to die."
        *   "I wish I were dead."
        *   "I'm going to kill myself."
        *   Talking about feeling hopeless or having no reason to live.
    *   **Indirect Verbal Cues:**
        *   "I'm a burden to everyone."
        *   "Things would be better if I wasn't here."
        *   "I just want the pain to end."
        *   Saying goodbye to people as if they won't see them again.
    *   **Behavioral Changes:**
        *   Increased substance use (alcohol, drugs).
        *   Withdrawing from friends, family, and activities.
        *   Sleeping too much or too little.
        *   Giving away prized possessions.
        *   Aggression, irritability, or agitation.
        *   Researching methods of suicide.
        *   Stockpiling pills or acquiring weapons.
        *   Sudden calmness or happiness after a period of depression (this can indicate a decision has been made).
    *   **Emotional Symptoms:**
        *   Depression, anxiety, or panic attacks.
        *   Hopelessness and feelings of being trapped.
        *   Intense shame, guilt, or self-hatred.
        *   Feeling like a burden to others.
        *   Loss of interest in things they used to enjoy.
        *   Extreme mood swings.
    *   **Situational Risk Factors:**
        *   History of suicide attempts (strongest predictor).
        *   Family history of suicide.
        *   Mental health disorders (depression, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, PTSD, anxiety disorders, personality disorders).
        *   Substance abuse.
        *   Serious physical illness or chronic pain.
        *   Recent loss (death of a loved one, job loss, relationship breakup).
        *   Social isolation or lack of support.
        *   Access to lethal means (firearms, medications).
        *   Exposure to suicide (knowing someone who died by suicide, media reports of suicide).
        *   Trauma or abuse (past or present).
        *   Financial difficulties.
        *   Legal troubles.
        *   Discrimination or marginalization.

**2. When to Seek Emergency Medical Help**

**IMMEDIATE ACTION IS REQUIRED IF:**

*   The individual has a **specific suicide plan** (method, time, place).
*   The individual has **access to the means** to carry out the plan (e.g., pills, weapon).
*   The individual has **already attempted suicide**.
*   The individual is **actively engaging in self-harm**.
*   The individual is **experiencing psychosis or severe agitation**.
*   There is an **immediate and imminent threat** to their safety or the safety of others.

**In these cases:**

*   **Call emergency services immediately (911 in the US, 112 in Europe, 000 in Australia, or your local emergency number).**
*   **Do not leave the person alone** until professional help arrives.
*   **Remove any immediate dangers** from the environment (weapons, medications, sharp objects).

**3. Step-by-Step First Aid Procedures**

*   **Step 1: Assess the Situation:**
    *   **Safety First:** Ensure your own safety and the safety of others.
    *   **Observe:** Look for immediate warning signs (as listed above). Listen carefully to what the person is saying and how they are behaving.
    *   **Do not be afraid to ask:** Direct, non-judgmental questions are important.  "Are you thinking about suicide?" "Are you having thoughts of hurting yourself?" Asking about suicide does not cause it; it opens a line of communication.
*   **Step 2: Engage and Listen:**
    *   **Active Listening:** Pay close attention to what the person is saying.  Reflect their feelings ("It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed").
    *   **Empathy:** Show compassion and understanding.  Avoid judgmental statements.  Acknowledge their pain.
    *   **Validate:** Validate their feelings, even if you don't understand them.  ("It sounds like you're going through a very difficult time").
    *   **Do not minimize:** Avoid saying things like "It's not that bad" or "Things will get better."  These statements can invalidate their feelings.
    *   **Do not argue or debate:** This is not the time to try to convince them that their problems are not real or that they should be happy.
*   **Step 3: Offer Reassurance and Hope:**
    *   **Reassure them that they are not alone.**
    *   **Remind them that help is available and that things can get better.**
    *   **Focus on small, achievable goals.**  ("Let's focus on getting through the next hour").
    *   **Help them identify reasons to live.**  (e.g., family, friends, pets, hobbies).
*   **Step 4: Remove Access to Lethal Means (If Possible and Safe):**
    *   If the person has a specific plan and access to the means, try to remove those means if it is safe to do so.
    *   This may involve removing weapons, medications, sharp objects, or other potentially harmful items from the immediate environment.
    *   **Important:** Do not put yourself in danger.  If you are unsure about your safety, call for emergency assistance.
*   **Step 5: Connect to Professional Help:**
    *   **Encourage them to seek professional help.**  Offer to help them find a therapist, counselor, or psychiatrist.
    *   **Provide them with resources:**
        *   **Suicide Prevention Lifeline:** Call or text 988 in the US and Canada. In the UK, call 111.
        *   **Crisis Text Line:** Text HOME to 741741.
        *   **The Trevor Project:** 1-866-488-7386 (for LGBTQ youth).
        *   **Local mental health services:** Search online for mental health resources in your area.
    *   **Offer to contact these resources with them or for them (with their permission).**
    *   **If they are unwilling to seek help, contact emergency services or a mental health professional yourself.**

**4. Home Remedies and Self-Care Tips (Supportive Measures, Not Replacements for Professional Help)**

These measures are primarily for the helper, to avoid burnout, and as supportive aftercare for the individual at risk *after* professional help is engaged. They are not a replacement for professional intervention.

*   **For the Individual at Risk (After Professional Help is Engaged):**
    *   **Create a Safety Plan:** Work with a mental health professional to develop a written safety plan that includes:
        *   Warning signs.
        *   Coping strategies.
        *   People to contact for support.
        *   Safe places to go.
        *   Steps to take in a crisis.
    *   **Limit Access to Social Media:** Social media can sometimes exacerbate feelings of isolation or hopelessness.
    *   **Engage in Gentle Exercise:** Physical activity can release endorphins and improve mood.
    *   **Practice Relaxation Techniques:** Deep breathing, meditation, or progressive muscle relaxation.
    *   **Maintain a Routine:** Regular sleep, meals, and activities can provide a sense of structure and stability.
    *   **Spend Time in Nature:** Exposure to nature can have a calming and restorative effect.
    *   **Connect with Supportive People:** Spend time with friends and family who provide emotional support.
    *   **Avoid Alcohol and Drugs:** These substances can worsen depression and increase impulsivity.
    *   **Journaling:** Writing down thoughts and feelings can help process emotions.
    *   **Engage in Hobbies:** Pursue activities that bring joy and satisfaction.
    *   **Mindfulness:** Practice being present in the moment and focusing on the senses.
*   **For the Helper (Preventing Burnout):**
    *   **Seek Support:** Talk to a trusted friend, family member, or therapist about your own feelings and experiences.
    *   **Set Boundaries:** It is important to protect your own mental and emotional well-being.  You are not responsible for "saving" someone.
    *   **Take Breaks:** Engage in activities that help you relax and recharge.
    *   **Practice Self-Care:** Get enough sleep, eat healthy foods, and exercise regularly.
    *   **Acknowledge Your Limits:** Recognize that you cannot do everything, and it is okay to ask for help.
    *   **Remember that you are doing your best, and you are making a difference.**

**5. Prevention Measures**

*   **Promote Mental Health Awareness:** Educate yourself and others about mental health disorders and suicide prevention.
*   **Reduce Stigma:** Challenge negative attitudes and stereotypes about mental illness.
*   **Encourage Help-Seeking Behavior:** Let people know that it is okay to ask for help and that resources are available.
*   **Provide Support to Vulnerable Individuals:** Reach out to people who are struggling with mental health issues, loss, or other difficult life circumstances.
*   **Promote Social Connectedness:** Encourage people to build and maintain strong relationships.
*   **Address Risk Factors:** Take steps to reduce risk factors for suicide, such as access to lethal means, substance abuse, and social isolation.
*   **Implement School-Based Prevention Programs:** Provide students with education about mental health and suicide prevention.
*   **Train Gatekeepers:** Train teachers, coaches, clergy, and other community members to recognize and respond to suicide risk.
*   **Limit Exposure to Suicide Contagion:** Be mindful of how suicide is portrayed in the media and avoid sensationalizing or romanticizing suicide.
*   **Promote Responsible Media Reporting:** Encourage media outlets to report on suicide in a way that is accurate, sensitive, and non-glamorizing.
*   **Support Research:** Invest in research to better understand the causes of suicide and develop effective prevention strategies.
*   **Advocate for Policies:** Support policies that promote mental health and reduce access to lethal means.

This guide is not exhaustive. It is essential to stay informed about the latest research and best practices in suicide prevention.  Remember, you are not alone, and help is available.
